\begin{abstract}
Graph-structured decomposition is a general strategy for organizing complex reasoning, but its effectiveness and diagnostic value in multi-hop question answering remain unclear across tasks. We study these questions using \textbf{GERS-DAG (Graph-Enhanced Reasoning System with DAG execution)}, which represents question decompositions as dependency DAGs and executes sub-questions in topological order. Our evaluation covers five LongBench subsets, full-context HotpotQA, a MuSiQue 4-hop Oracle study, and a 2WikiMultiHopQA error analysis, with a Qwen-Plus study providing limited cross-model evidence. Under Qwen3-8B, paired bootstrap intervals favor GERS-DAG over CoT-SC on LongBench \texttt{multifieldqa\_en} ($\Delta$F1 $=+0.070$, 95\% CI $[+0.007,+0.132]$) and \texttt{musique} ($+0.063$, $[+0.001,+0.124]$), while estimated effects are null, negative, or inconclusive in the remaining end-task settings. In the Oracle study, gold decomposition improves F1 by +0.091 over model decomposition and by +0.075 over CoT-SC, identifying decomposition quality as a key bottleneck. Separately, \textbf{BiCheck} re-derives sub-answers after generation and raises correctness AUROC from 0.498 to 0.589 and correct/wrong score separation from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$. However, many incorrect answers remain self-consistent. These results show that graph decomposition is regime-dependent and that BiCheck is a weak diagnostic rather than a correctness verifier.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Multi-hop question answering requires combining evidence from multiple passages and is central to evaluating LLM reasoning. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting~\citep{wei2022cot} elicits intermediate steps; Self-Consistency (CoT-SC)~\citep{wang2023sc} samples multiple paths and takes the majority answer. Both are strong baselines, but long multi-document contexts---as in retrieval-augmented generation over multi-domain corpora and benchmarks such as LongBench~\citep{bai2024longbench}---introduce two potential difficulties:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Cross-passage evidence selection.} When relevant evidence is scattered across thousands of tokens, a single pass must locate and connect facts among distractors.
\item \textbf{Quality-agnostic voting.} CoT-SC weights sampled answers equally; with three samples, two agreeing but unsupported answers can determine the majority over one correct answer.
\end{enumerate}

Graph-structured reasoning is a general family of methods rather than a technique specific to multi-hop QA. GoT~\citep{besta2024got} allows thoughts to be merged and distilled; RwG~\citep{han2025rwg} structures context as an entity-relation graph; MoDeGraph~\citep{oruche2025modegraph} constructs a dependency graph through prompting. We use multi-hop QA as an evaluation setting in which sub-question dependencies and cross-passage evidence integration can be measured explicitly. This motivates two empirical questions: when topologically executed question decomposition helps across paired QA benchmarks, and whether explicit sub-question units support a useful content-consistency diagnostic. In our experiments, a purely structural graph-validity score is nearly non-discriminative because generated graphs are usually legal DAGs.

We separate answer generation from diagnosis. \textbf{GERS-DAG (Graph-Enhanced Reasoning System with DAG execution)} decomposes the question, executes the dependency graph, and aggregates sub-answers into final answer $A$. \textbf{BiCheck} then independently re-answers each sub-question using $A$ and the context as conditioning information, and measures agreement with the forward sub-answers. BiCheck is post-hoc in the evaluated single-path configuration, so end-task comparisons measure GERS-DAG while the diagnostic experiments evaluate BiCheck. The explicit DAG provides named sub-question units on which this content-level analysis can operate; the graph edges themselves are not reversed.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \emph{Cross-benchmark evidence for graph-structured decomposition.} Paired evaluation favors GERS-DAG on two of five LongBench subsets, while the remaining subsets, HotpotQA, and 2WikiMultiHopQA identify settings where the advantage is absent, negative, or inconclusive.
\item \emph{Controlled Oracle interventions.} On MuSiQue 4-hop, gold decomposition improves the same pipeline over model decomposition by +0.091 F1 and exceeds CoT-SC by +0.075 F1, estimating the headroom available from more accurate decomposition.
\item \emph{A content-consistency diagnostic.} BiCheck raises correct/wrong discrimination from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$ and AUROC from 0.498 to 0.589, exposing both the weakness of structural graph scores and the persistence of self-consistent errors.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\noindent\textbf{Chain-of-Thought reasoning.} \citet{wei2022cot} introduced CoT prompting; \citet{wang2023sc} proposed Self-Consistency (CoT-SC), whose equal-weight majority vote does not exploit path quality. \citet{yao2023tot} proposed Tree of Thoughts (ToT) with search over thought states, without explicit sub-question dependency modeling. These methods are based on linear or tree structures.

\noindent\textbf{Graph-structured reasoning and decomposition.} \citet{besta2024got} models general problem solving as a graph of thoughts that supports merging and distillation. \citet{han2025rwg} uses a context graph as input, while \citet{oruche2025modegraph} constructs a multi-hop dependency graph through prompting. These works establish graph structures as general reasoning or context-organization tools. GERS-DAG instantiates this broader idea as explicit sub-question DAG execution for multi-hop QA; BiCheck analyzes the resulting sub-answers after generation rather than changing the answer-producing path.

\noindent\textbf{Long-context QA benchmarks.} LongBench~\citep{bai2024longbench} is a bilingual multi-task long-context benchmark; MuSiQue~\citep{trivedi2022musique} composes single-hop questions into 2--4-hop compositions with gold sub-question decompositions. We use LongBench multi-document QA subsets as the main benchmark, and MuSiQue for controlled Oracle mechanism analysis.

\noindent\textbf{Consistency checking and self-evaluation.} Process Reward Models~\citep{lightman2024verify} score intermediate steps but require large amounts of labeled data. Self-Refine~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine} iteratively refines answers via self-feedback on linear text. BiCheck instead measures agreement between forward and post-hoc re-derived answers over explicit DAG sub-questions, without labeled verifier training.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

Given question $Q$ and context $C$, GERS-DAG: (1) decomposes $Q$ into sub-questions $\{q_i\}$ with dependencies; (2) builds DAG $G=(V,E)$; (3) obtains a topological order $\tau$; (4) executes sub-questions in that order while passing predecessor answers to dependent nodes; and (5) aggregates the sub-answers into final answer $A$ using confidence annotations. BiCheck is a separate sixth stage that computes the post-hoc score $S = 0.3S_{\text{struct}} + 0.7S_{\text{crossval}}$. Figure~\ref{fig:framework} depicts both components. The released \texttt{gers\_cv2\_fullctx} configuration executes them together, but BiCheck does not change $A$ in the main single-path protocol.

\subsection{Reasoning-State Graph}

Nodes: \emph{FactNode} (facts/original question), \emph{StepNode} (sub-question and answer), \emph{ConclusionNode} (final conclusion). Edges: \emph{DeriveEdge}, \emph{SupportEdge}, \emph{ConflictEdge}. The LLM decomposes $Q$ into an ordered sub-question list with dependencies; nodes and edges are created accordingly. Topological sort orders execution; cycles are broken first.

\subsection{Topological Execution and Answer Aggregation}

The decomposer returns structured sub-questions with an identifier, an operation type, and a \texttt{depends\_on} list. Dependency declarations become directed edges between StepNodes; sub-questions without predecessors are connected to the original FactNode. A topological ordering then guarantees that every required predecessor has been answered before a dependent sub-question is executed. For each StepNode, the prompt includes the original question, the shared evidence context, and the answers of its immediate predecessors. This design makes dependency transmission explicit rather than relying on their accidental ordering in a linear rationale.

After all StepNodes have been executed, GERS-DAG aggregates the ordered sub-question--answer trace into $A$. The aggregation prompt requires the output entity type to match the original question, preventing intermediate entities such as dates or directors from replacing the requested film, person, or place. A lightweight confidence heuristic additionally marks uncertain or weakly grounded sub-answers as \texttt{[LOW CONFIDENCE]}; these annotations inform aggregation but do not alter graph execution. These mechanisms belong to the answer-producing pipeline and are evaluated separately from post-hoc BiCheck.

\subsection{BiCheck: Sub-Answer Re-Derivation Diagnostics}

\noindent\textbf{Structural score (baseline).}
$S_{\text{struct}} = w_1 C_{\text{conn}} + w_2 C_{\text{cycle}} + w_3 C_{\text{cov}}$
with $w_1{=}w_3{=}0.35, w_2{=}0.30$ for connectivity, acyclicity, and coverage. On our data this metric clusters (mean 0.66, discrimination $-0.0035$) and cannot distinguish reasoning quality.

\noindent\textbf{Forward/re-derived cross-check.} Given forward sub-answers $\{a_i\}$ and final answer $A$, BiCheck independently re-answers each sub-question after the forward pass, conditioned on $A$ and context $C$, yielding re-derived answers $\{a'_i\}$. Graph edges are not reversed. Content consistency is
\begin{equation}
S_{\text{crossval}} = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} w_i \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{match}(a_i, a'_i)]}{\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} w_i}
\end{equation}
where $i\in\{0,\ldots,n-1\}$ and $w_i = 0.5 + 0.5(i+1)/n$, assigning more weight to later nodes because they integrate more preceding reasoning and are closer to the final answer. The binary match function returns 1 when normalized exact match, substring containment, or normalized numeric equality holds, and 0 otherwise. The prompt instructs the model to derive from context rather than copy $A$ to reduce confirmation bias.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/image3}
\caption{BiCheck diagnostic. After the GERS-DAG answer is fixed, each sub-question is re-answered using $A$ and the context; agreement yields a content-consistency score.}
\label{fig:crosscheck}

\noindent\textbf{Confidence-weighted aggregation.} Each sub-answer is scored by a lightweight heuristic (non-emptiness, absence of uncertainty markers, entity grounding in context, pure-number check); sub-answers below $\theta_{\text{conf}}{=}0.5$ are tagged \texttt{[LOW CONFIDENCE]} in the aggregation prompt.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\noindent\textbf{LongBench English QA.} We evaluate \texttt{multifieldqa\_en} (100 attempted examples), \texttt{musique} (200 attempted examples), and \texttt{narrativeqa} (100 attempted examples), then extend the exploratory analysis with \texttt{qasper} and \texttt{2wikimqa}. The first two show positive paired differences; \texttt{narrativeqa} has high failure rates and is treated as inconclusive.

\noindent\textbf{Mechanism benchmark.} MuSiQue~\citep{trivedi2022musique} 4-hop subset (first 200 samples with hop=4 from a seed=42 shuffle of the \texttt{musique\_ans\_v1.0\_dev} split) for the controlled Oracle study, since gold sub-question decompositions and supporting-fact annotations are available.

\noindent\textbf{Regime-boundary benchmark.} HotpotQA~\citep{yang2018hotpotqa} n=500 (single-file dense evidence, mean $\sim$4.7k characters) as a documented boundary where decomposition does not help.

\noindent\textbf{Baselines.} CoT-SC ($N{=}3$, majority vote) is the primary paired-comparison baseline. Standard CoT and Zero-Shot appear in ablations.

\noindent\textbf{Model and protocol.} We use Qwen3-8B through the DashScope API with four workers. Both methods receive the same full LongBench context, and all methods share answer extraction and normalization. We attempted 200 MuSiQue and 100 NarrativeQA examples; paired analyses retain only examples for which both methods returned valid outputs, yielding n=193 and n=71. CoT-SC/GERS-DAG produced 7/2 failures on MuSiQue and 21/26 on NarrativeQA. We report paired bootstrap 95\% CIs ($B=10{,}000$, seed=42) and McNemar tests on EM correctness. These dataset-level tests are exploratory and are not adjusted across datasets and metrics; differential failure rates are analyzed separately.

\noindent\textbf{Observed improvements.} GERS-DAG improves F1 over CoT-SC by +0.070 on \texttt{multifieldqa\_en} and +0.063 on \texttt{musique}; both paired bootstrap intervals exclude zero (Table~\ref{tab:main}). These dataset-specific results support graph decomposition for moderately long multi-passage contexts, with the MuSiQue estimate carrying greater uncertainty because its lower bound is close to zero.

\noindent\textbf{Exploratory interpretation.} The two positive subsets differ in length but both contain multiple passages and distractors. One plausible explanation is that decomposition narrows the reasoning focus for each step, even though the full context remains available. Because the subset grouping was developed from the observed results, it should be treated as a hypothesis for future preregistered evaluation rather than a confirmed regime law.

\noindent\textbf{Observed limitations.} On \texttt{narrativeqa}, both methods score F1${<}0.24$ and have substantial, unequal timeout rates. This experiment is inconclusive about ultra-long narratives under a more reliable serving configuration. On HotpotQA (Section~\ref{sec:boundary}), CoT-SC modestly outperforms GERS-DAG under fair full-context evaluation.

\noindent\emph{(1) Accurate decomposition has measurable potential value.} Gold decomposition improves the same pipeline over model-generated decomposition by $+0.091$ F1 (n=199 paired valid outputs, 95\% CI $[+0.041,+0.142]$, McNemar $p{=}0.002$). This Oracle comparison estimates GERS-DAG headroom rather than deployable automatic performance.

\noindent\emph{(2) Gold sub-answers give the largest measured Oracle improvement.} Relative to the gold-decomposition condition, substituting gold sub-answers raises F1 from 0.439 to 0.839, whereas substituting gold supporting passages raises it to 0.514. Because these interventions are not cumulative and can interact, their differences are not additive percentages of total error. \subsection{BiCheck: Structural $\to$ Content Diagnostics}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/image2}

\label{fig:cs}

On the HotpotQA diagnostic run, the structural score clusters (correct mean 0.6592, wrong mean 0.6628, discrimination $-0.0035$, AUROC 0.498). BiCheck raises discrimination to $+0.0847$ and AUROC to 0.589, turning the score into a weak content self-consistency signal (Figure~\ref{fig:cs}).

\noindent\textbf{Self-consistency has a ceiling.} Among 250 samples with CS${=}1.0$, 156 are EM-wrong: fluent hallucinations can agree with themselves. BiCheck therefore provides a diagnostic signal rather than a demonstrated correctness verifier or selection mechanism; grounded verification remains open (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Regime Boundary Analysis}
\label{sec:boundary}

We report documented regimes where GERS-DAG does \emph{not} outperform CoT-SC and analyze why.

\noindent\textbf{Boundary 1: HotpotQA under full context.} On HotpotQA n=500, CoT-SC outperforms GERS-DAG in F1 (0.716 vs.\ 0.683; difference $+0.032$ in favor of CoT-SC, 95\% CI $[+0.002,+0.063]$), while the EM difference is not significant ($p{=}0.314$). A separate legacy truncated-context run contains per-type EM results, but those values are not comparable with this full-context experiment and are omitted from the main analysis.

\noindent\textbf{Boundary 2: deep bridge-composition.} On the 2WikiMultiHopQA~\citep{ho20202wiki} n=100 diagnostic, GERS-DAG is slightly below Standard CoT on comparison questions (F1 0.775 vs.\ 0.815) and substantially below it on \emph{bridge\_comparison} (0.524 vs.\ 0.905). An early entity error can propagate through the bridge$\to$compare chain (Figure~\ref{fig:type}). BiCheck can remain high when forward and re-derived answers share the same error, limiting its coverage of this failure mode.

\subsection{Implementation Diagnostics and Cost}

The main configuration uses model-generated DAG execution, confidence annotations during aggregation, and post-hoc BiCheck. Confidence weighting changes the aggregation prompt; its measured effect was small (+0.004 EM in a legacy ablation). The full GERS-DAG+BiCheck pipeline uses approximately 6--8 LLM calls per question versus 3 for CoT-SC. Under the four-worker API configuration, mean wall-clock latency was 6.6s for the full pipeline and 8.3s for CoT-SC; these are shared-system measurements, not serial model latency. When only the final answer is required, BiCheck and its re-derivation calls can be disabled.

\subsection{Cross-Model Generalization and Auxiliary Ablations}

\noindent\textbf{Qwen-Plus on HotpotQA (n=300).} GERS-DAG obtains EM 0.363 / F1 0.496 and CoT-SC obtains EM 0.367 / F1 0.494 (EM difference $-0.004$, McNemar $p{=}1.000$; F1 difference $+0.002$). Because this evaluation is on HotpotQA rather than the LongBench subsets, it does not establish how model scaling affects the target setting.

\noindent\textbf{Auxiliary diagnostics.} (i) Uniform and position-weighted cross-check scores yield similar legacy-run answers, as expected because scoring occurs after answer generation. (ii) A context-only re-derivation removes $A$ from the diagnostic anchor and produces a lower CS while leaving answers essentially unchanged. (iii) An evidence-grounded variant suppresses inflated CS (0.781$\to$0.730) while preserving EM in the diagnostic sample.

\subsection{Additional Diagnostic Experiments}

We report additional experiments that probe the boundaries of GERS-DAG and BiCheck.

\noindent\textbf{Two additional LongBench subsets.} On \texttt{qasper} (n=200), GERS-DAG shows a non-significant +0.018 F1 difference (95\% CI $[-0.016,+0.054]$). On \texttt{2wikimqa} (n=195 paired valid outputs), it shows a non-significant $-0.021$ difference (95\% CI $[-0.076,+0.035]$). These exploratory results are consistent with, but do not establish, the moderate-length multi-passage hypothesis.

\noindent\textbf{Symmetric context-budget audit.} On HotpotQA with seven symmetric truncation budgets (n=200 each), the largest difference is $+0.060$ F1 at budget 2000 ($p{=}0.054$), with no statistically detectable difference at the other six budgets. This reduces, but does not eliminate, concern that context-budget effects contribute to the LongBench observations.

\noindent\textbf{MuSiQue hop-axis.} With model decomposition, GERS-DAG does not outperform CoT-SC at 2, 3, or 4 hops ($\Delta$F1 $=-0.008,-0.062,-0.022$; all non-significant). The paired Oracle comparison in Section~\ref{sec:oracle} shows a +0.091 F1 improvement when model decomposition is replaced by gold decomposition within the pipeline.

\noindent\textbf{Rejected variants (design-space audit).} (a) Stepwise NLI verification with re-answer (EASV) adds only +0.004 F1 over its Oracle-1 baseline. (b) Iterative deepening decomposition acts at the leaf layer, whereas compression occurs during the initial depth decision. (c) Per-sub-question BM25 retrieval obtains F1 0.563 versus 0.683 for full-context GERS-DAG on HotpotQA ($\Delta$F1 $=-0.121$, $p{<}0.001$). (d) Grounded-forward gating underperforms the standard pipeline in an n=20 smoke test. These negative results delimit the tested design space.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\noindent\textbf{Content self-consistency is not correctness.} Even after BiCheck, 156 of 250 CS${=}1.0$ HotpotQA samples are EM-wrong and AUROC is 0.589. The modest improvement over chance is itself mechanistically informative: semantic self-checking improves on structural legality but reaches a ceiling when fluent errors remain self-consistent. Evidence-span extraction or an independent verifier is needed before using this signal for reliable selection.

\noindent\textbf{Confirmation bias.} Anchoring re-derivation on $A$ risks spurious agreement. We mitigate via (i) explicit ``derive from context'' prompt instruction, (ii) a context-only ablation showing near-identical end-task performance. Adversarial answer perturbation is future work.

\noindent\textbf{Cross-model evidence is limited.} The Qwen-Plus experiment is confined to HotpotQA, where GERS-DAG shows no advantage. Evaluation on the LongBench subsets is required before drawing conclusions about model scaling.

\noindent\textbf{Deep-bridge error propagation.} An early sub-answer error can propagate and invert the final answer on 2Wiki bridge-comparison questions. Re-derivation anchored on an incorrect $A$ can still self-agree, limiting diagnostic coverage.

\noindent\textbf{Depth-planning failure and future work.} Our decomposition analysis reveals that 67\% of MuSiQue 4-hop questions are compressed into ${\leq}3$-hop decompositions by current 8B LLMs. This depth-planning failure at the initial decomposition step is a candidate target for future work (depth-prompted decomposition, few-shot 4-hop gold exemplars, decomposition self-consistency over depth).

\noindent\textbf{Sample-size caveat.} LongBench provides $n{=}100$/$n{=}200$ for the target subsets; larger multi-domain long-context benchmarks would tighten confidence intervals. This is the standard LongBench evaluation size and is the natural next step for scaling.

\section{Conclusion}

We evaluated graph-structured question decomposition for multi-hop QA while separating answer generation from post-hoc diagnosis. Across five LongBench subsets and additional HotpotQA, MuSiQue, and 2WikiMultiHopQA analyses, GERS-DAG improves paired F1 over CoT-SC in two LongBench settings, while the remaining settings identify regimes where the estimated effect is null, negative, or inconclusive. Oracle interventions show that gold decomposition improves the pipeline by +0.091 F1 and that gold sub-answers provide the largest measured intervention gain, locating two concrete sources of headroom. BiCheck converts a non-discriminative structural score into a weak content-consistency diagnostic (discrimination $+0.0847$, AUROC 0.589), but self-consistent errors remain common. Together, these results locate both the opportunity and the limits of applying graph decomposition to multi-hop QA: accurate planning and sub-answer generation matter, while self-agreement alone is insufficient for verification.
