\begin{abstract}
Some graph-enhanced reasoning variants use structural graph-validity metrics (connectivity, acyclicity, coverage) as consistency checks, but such metrics may not reflect reasoning correctness. In our structural-CS variant, the Consistency Score is weakly discriminative between correct and incorrect reasoning, and multi-path selection can degenerate toward random sampling. We propose GERS, which models reasoning as a dependency DAG executed in topological order. The core contribution is \textbf{bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking}: using the final answer and context as an anchor, each sub-question is re-derived in reverse and the forward/backward sub-answers are compared, upgrading the Consistency Score from a structural-validity metric into a content self-consistency signal. On 500 HotpotQA samples, this raises the score's correct/wrong discrimination from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$ and AUROC from 0.498 to about 0.58--0.59. The highest point-estimate configuration GERS-CV2 achieves EM=0.302, F1=0.413, improving over CoT-SC by 4 F1 points; this gain is significant under both a paired McNemar test on EM correctness ($p=0.029$) and paired bootstrap 95\% CIs that exclude zero, and GERS-CV2 also significantly outperforms a MoDeGraph-style graph-prompt baseline ($p=0.010$). We explicitly show that self-consistency is not correctness: many high-CS answers remain wrong, and 2WikiMultiHopQA exposes a boundary on deep bridge-comparison questions where upstream entity errors propagate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Multi-hop reasoning requires models to combine evidence across multiple fragments. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting \citep{wei2022cot} improves LLM performance by eliciting intermediate steps, but standard CoT organizes reasoning as \textbf{linear text} with two limitations: (1) \emph{missing non-linear dependencies}---branching, merging, or parallel dependencies among sub-questions cannot be expressed, so a DAG-shaped path is linearized and structural information is lost; and (2) \emph{quality-agnostic voting}---CoT-SC \citep{wang2023sc} samples multiple paths and takes the majority answer with equal-weight votes that cannot exploit path quality.

Recent works enhance reasoning with graph structures: GoT \citep{besta2024got} proposes graph-of-thoughts with merging/distillation but does not impose sub-question topological execution; RwG \citep{han2025rwg} structures context into an entity-relation graph; MoDeGraph \citep{oruche2025modegraph} builds a multi-hop dependency graph as a prompt method. GERS instead studies whether graph-derived consistency scores can serve as diagnostic and weak candidate-quality signals when the graph participates in sub-question execution and checking.

We propose \textbf{GERS}, which models reasoning as a dependency DAG executed in topological order. We find that a Consistency Score computed purely from graph-theoretic properties is ineffective: LLM-generated decomposition graphs are almost always legal DAGs, so scores cluster (around 0.6--0.7) and multi-path selection degenerates to random sampling (discrimination only $-0.0035$ on 500 samples). We therefore propose the core innovation \textbf{bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking}: using the final answer and context as an anchor, each sub-question is re-derived in reverse and the forward/backward sub-answers are compared, upgrading the Consistency Score from a structural-validity metric into a content self-consistency signal. This cross-check is naturally supported by explicit DAG decomposition---which provides named sub-question units and dependency edges---and is less straightforward to apply to unstructured linear CoT. Rather than claiming uniform superiority, this work identifies and fixes a key bottleneck: structural graph validity is not equivalent to reasoning correctness. Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Mechanism (core)}: Bidirectional cross-checking raises the Consistency Score's correct/wrong discrimination from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$ and AUROC from 0.498 to about 0.58--0.59, turning consistency checking from a structural metric into a weak but useful content self-consistency signal.
\item \textbf{Empirical}: The highest point-estimate configuration GERS-CV2 achieves EM=0.302, F1=0.413 on HotpotQA (500 samples), a +4pt F1 difference over CoT-SC that is significant under both a paired McNemar test ($p=0.029$) and paired bootstrap CIs excluding zero; it also significantly outperforms a MoDeGraph-style baseline ($p=0.010$).
\item \textbf{Diagnostic}: A boundary analysis on 2WikiMultiHopQA shows the method suits comparison-style decomposition but fails on deep bridge-comparison questions where upstream errors propagate. The tested structural-score graph-level self-consistency yields no extra gain.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\noindent\textbf{Chain-of-Thought reasoning.} \citet{wei2022cot} proposed CoT prompting. \citet{wang2023sc} proposed Self-Consistency (CoT-SC), whose equal-weight voting does not exploit path quality. \citet{yao2023tot} proposed Tree of Thoughts (ToT), with no explicit dependency modeling among sub-questions. These methods are based on linear or tree structures.

\noindent\textbf{Graph-enhanced reasoning.} GoT \citep{besta2024got} models reasoning as a graph for merging/distillation but focuses on prompt engineering. RwG \citep{han2025rwg} uses a context graph as a one-time input. MoDeGraph \citep{oruche2025modegraph} builds a multi-hop dependency graph but remains a prompt method. GERS couples the reasoning graph with execution and studies graph-derived consistency as a diagnostic and weak ranking signal.

\noindent\textbf{Consistency checking and self-evaluation.} Process Reward Models \citep{lightman2024verify} score intermediate steps but require large amounts of labeled data. Self-Refine \citep{madaan2023selfrefine} iteratively refines answers via self-feedback but operates on linear text without a separately checkable sub-question structure. GERS uses graph-theoretic algorithms for structural checking and designs bidirectional cross-checking so that consistency scores can be analyzed and used in controlled variants. A qualitative positioning table against these families is given in the supplement.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

GERS has three modules: reasoning-state graph representation, graph-constrained chain generation, and consistency checking. The flow (Figure~1): input $Q,C$ $\to$ decompose into sub-questions $\{q_i\}$ with dependencies $\to$ build DAG $G=(V,E)$ $\to$ topological sort $\tau$ $\to$ execute step-by-step to get sub-answers $a_i$ $\to$ aggregate into final answer $A$ (answer-type alignment) $\to$ compute the cross-validated consistency score $S = 0.3\cdot S_{\text{struct}} + 0.7\cdot S_{\text{crossval}}$ $\to$ output $A, G, S$. We also evaluate a GERS-SC diagnostic baseline that samples $K$ DAGs and selects by the original structural CS.

\subsection{Reasoning-State Graph Representation}

Nodes: FactNode (facts/original question), StepNode (sub-question and answer), ConclusionNode (final conclusion). Edges: DeriveEdge ($u\to v$), SupportEdge, ConflictEdge. The LLM decomposes $Q$ into an ordered sub-question list with dependencies, and nodes/edges are created accordingly.

\subsection{Graph-Constrained Chain Generation}

Topological sort gives execution order $\tau=(v_1,\dots,v_n)$; cycles are broken first, so each sub-question is answered only after its predecessors. Sub-questions are answered in topological order with each answer passed as context to dependent sub-questions (dependency propagation), then the chain is aggregated into $A$. \textbf{Answer-type alignment} constrains $A$ to match the entity type requested by the original question (e.g., ``which film'' $\to$ a film name, not an intermediate director), which is critical for bridging-comparison questions.

\subsection{Consistency Checking and Bidirectional Cross-Validation}

\noindent\textbf{Structural Consistency Score (baseline).}
\begin{equation}
S_{\text{struct}} = w_1\cdot C_{\text{conn}} + w_2\cdot C_{\text{cycle}} + w_3\cdot C_{\text{cov}}
\end{equation}
with $w_1=w_3=0.35, w_2=0.30$ for connectivity, acyclicity, coverage, plus a chain-length penalty. LLM-generated graphs are almost always legal connected acyclic DAGs, so $S_{\text{struct}}$ clusters (mean 0.662, std 0.098, discrimination $-0.0035$) and cannot distinguish reasoning quality.

\noindent\textbf{Sub-answer bidirectional cross-validation (core).} Given forward sub-answers $\{a_i\}$ and final answer $A$, each sub-question is re-answered in reverse using ``$A$ + context $C$'' as the anchor, yielding backward sub-answers $\{a'_i\}$:

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/image3}
\caption{Bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking: each sub-question is re-derived in reverse from the answer+context; forward/backward agreement yields a content self-consistency score that replaces the non-discriminative structural score.}
\end{figure}
\begin{equation}
S_{\text{crossval}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i\cdot\mathbb{1}[\text{match}(a_i, a'_i)]}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}
\end{equation}
where $w_i = 0.5 + 0.5\cdot(i+1)/n$ gives downstream sub-questions higher weight, as their errors propagate further. The match function is a three-level heuristic: (i) exact match after normalization (lowercasing, punctuation removal, yes/no and numeric unification); (ii) substring containment or numeric equality; (iii) otherwise 0. An optional LLM judge (disabled by default for cost) handles residual semantic cases. The reverse prompt requires ``re-derive from the context, do not copy the final answer'' to mitigate confirmation bias. This cross-check is naturally supported by explicit DAG decomposition and is more interpretable than applying it to unstructured CoT.

\noindent\textbf{Confirmation-bias control.} A context-only backward variant that removes the answer anchor obtains EM 0.300 / F1 0.414 with lower CS 0.646, close to the default EM 0.302 / F1 0.413 / CS 0.777, suggesting the end-task gain is not driven by simply feeding the final answer back.

\noindent\textbf{New Consistency Score.} The layers are combined with fixed weights (set heuristically, held constant across all experiments, no test-set tuning):
\begin{equation}
S = 0.3\cdot S_{\text{struct}} + 0.7\cdot S_{\text{crossval}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Confidence-Weighted Aggregation}

Each sub-answer confidence is estimated by a lightweight heuristic (no extra LLM call): non-emptiness, absence of uncertainty signals, entity grounding in context, and pure-number check. Sub-answers below $\theta_{\text{conf}}=0.5$ are tagged \texttt{[LOW CONFIDENCE]} in the aggregation prompt, reducing single-step error propagation.

\subsection{Graph-Level Self-Consistency (GERS-SC)}

GERS-SC generates $K$ DAGs (decomposition temperatures $T\in\{0.3,0.5,0.7\}$) and selects the highest-scoring one. The main-table variant uses the original structural CS for selection; its failure motivates bidirectional cross-validation. Full multi-path selection with the new cross-validated score is left for future work.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

HotpotQA \citep{yang2018hotpotqa} (500 samples: 404 bridge + 96 comparison) is the main benchmark. 2WikiMultiHopQA \citep{ho20202wiki} is used as a boundary analysis (100-sample typed diagnostic split plus a 300-sample larger check, reported in the supplement). Baselines: Zero-Shot, Standard CoT, CoT-SC ($N=3$), CoT-SC+GERS rerank (ablation), and a MoDeGraph-style graph-prompt baseline---an unofficial implementation from the public method description, not an official reproduction. All methods use a unified concise answer extraction/normalization pipeline. Our methods: GERS+adaptive (DAG execution + adaptive routing); GERS-SC (+graph-level self-consistency $K=3$); GERS-CV (+bidirectional cross-validation); GERS-CV2 (+confidence weighting, highest point estimate). Model: Qwen3-8B (DashScope API), 4-worker parallel, zero failures. Metrics: EM, F1, Consistency Score. Main comparisons report paired bootstrap 95\% CIs (10000 resamples of per-sample differences) and McNemar tests on paired EM correctness. To reduce evaluation-pipeline distortions we unify three treatments: fix an EM bidirectional-substring bug, use the same extraction/normalization code for all methods, and add answer-type alignment in GERS aggregation.

\subsection{Main Results}

GERS-CV2 has the highest point-estimate EM=0.302, F1=0.413, with a +4pt F1 difference over CoT-SC. It also significantly outperforms the MoDeGraph-style baseline (F1 0.366; McNemar $p=0.010$, paired F1 CI $[+0.012,+0.083]$). Bidirectional cross-checking (0.284$\to$0.298) is associated with a +1.4pt EM change. The CoT-SC+GERS rerank (0.372) is on par with CoT-SC (0.373), so applying graph scores post hoc to already-generated linear CoT answers is insufficient. Zero-Shot (0.276) is already close to GERS on HotpotQA---its context directly contains answer evidence, compressing the advantage of structured methods (analyzed in Section~4.4).

\subsection{Case Study}

\textbf{Case A (self-consistent, correct).} ``Were Scott Derrickson and Ed Wood of the same nationality?'' Forward decomposes into two nationality sub-questions, both ``American'' $\to$ ``yes''. Reverse verification, anchored on ``yes''+context, independently re-derives ``American''/``American'', matching forward; crossval=1.0.

\textbf{Case B (inconsistent, hallucination caught).} ``Which director had the longest career, Alain Resnais or Scott Sidney?'' (gold: Scott Sidney). Forward hallucinates birth/death years (1922, 2014, 1902, 1975) absent from the context and concludes ``Alain Resnais''. Reverse verification, re-deriving from context, returns ``not in context'' for all 7 sub-questions; crossval drops to 0.0. The old structural CS stays 0.7 (legal DAG) and detects nothing; the new CS reflects the content inconsistency.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

\noindent\textbf{Core value.} The main contribution is raising CS discrimination from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$ (AUROC 0.498$\to$0.58--0.59), upgrading consistency checking from ``is the graph legal'' to ``is the reasoning content self-consistent''. The EM gain is modest and the score is still a weak correctness predictor; its value is diagnostic and ranking-oriented.

\noindent\textbf{Self-consistency is not correctness.} Raising the cross-validation score can make a chain more internally self-consistent without making it more correct: CS=1.0 still contains many EM-wrong answers, and repair variants raise CS while reducing EM/F1 (supplement). Bidirectional cross-checking is a content-consistency signal, not a complete correctness verifier.

\noindent\textbf{Honest positioning.} GERS-CV2 is significantly better than CoT-SC, Standard CoT, and the MoDeGraph-style baseline on HotpotQA EM under paired evaluation (McNemar $p\le0.040$; CIs excluding zero), but the gain is small in magnitude (+4pt F1) because HotpotQA's context directly contains answer evidence.

\noindent\textbf{Limitations.} On bridge\_comparison, GERS's error propagation lags behind linear CoT---an inherent limitation of graph decomposition on deep multi-hop. The MoDeGraph-style baseline is an unofficial implementation; the comparison covers one prompt-only graph baseline, not all graph-reasoning systems.

\section{Conclusion}

We propose GERS, whose core innovation is \textbf{bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking}: re-deriving sub-questions in reverse from the answer+context and comparing forward/backward consistency, upgrading the Consistency Score from a pure graph-theoretic metric (discrimination $-0.0035$, AUROC 0.498) to a more informative content self-consistency signal ($+0.0847$, AUROC 0.58--0.59), naturally supported by explicit DAG decomposition. GERS-CV2 achieves EM=0.302, F1=0.413 on 500 HotpotQA samples, a +4pt F1 difference over CoT-SC that is significant under both a paired McNemar test ($p=0.029$) and paired bootstrap CIs excluding zero, and a significant improvement over a MoDeGraph-style baseline ($p=0.010$). We also provide a boundary analysis showing the method suits comparison-style decomposition but fails on deep bridge-comparison questions. GERS should be read as a step toward interpretable graph-based reasoning diagnostics, not a solved correctness verifier.
