\begin{abstract}
Long multi-document question answering requires identifying evidence scattered across passages while ignoring substantial distractor content. We evaluate \textbf{GERS-CV2}, a pipeline that decomposes a question into a dependency DAG, executes sub-questions in topological order, and computes a post-hoc content-consistency diagnostic through bidirectional sub-answer re-derivation. Under our Qwen3-8B protocol, nominal paired tests favor GERS-CV2 over CoT-SC on two LongBench subsets: \texttt{multifieldqa\_en} (n=100, $\Delta$F1 $=+0.070$, 95\% CI $[+0.007,+0.132]$) and \texttt{musique} (n=193, $\Delta$F1 $=+0.063$, 95\% CI $[+0.001,+0.124]$). These tests are not corrected for comparisons across datasets and metrics. On MuSiQue 4-hop, supplying gold decomposition improves the pipeline over model decomposition by +0.091 F1 and over CoT-SC by +0.075 F1, estimating the potential value of accurate decomposition. The cross-check improves Consistency Score discrimination from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$ (AUROC 0.498$\to$0.589), but does not alter the answer in the evaluated single-path configuration and remains vulnerable to self-consistent errors. Results on HotpotQA, NarrativeQA, and Qwen-Plus delimit the scope of the observed gains.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Multi-hop question answering requires combining evidence from multiple passages and is central to evaluating LLM reasoning. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting~\citep{wei2022cot} elicits intermediate steps; Self-Consistency (CoT-SC)~\citep{wang2023sc} samples multiple paths and takes the majority answer. Both are strong baselines, but long multi-document contexts---as in retrieval-augmented generation over multi-domain corpora and benchmarks such as LongBench~\citep{bai2024longbench}---introduce two potential difficulties:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Cross-passage evidence selection.} When relevant evidence is scattered across thousands of tokens, a single pass must locate and connect facts among distractors.
\item \textbf{Quality-agnostic voting.} CoT-SC weights sampled answers equally; with three samples, two agreeing but unsupported answers can determine the majority over one correct answer.
\end{enumerate}

Graph-based reasoning has been proposed as an alternative: GoT~\citep{besta2024got} allows merging and distillation of thoughts; RwG~\citep{han2025rwg} structures context into an entity-relation graph; MoDeGraph~\citep{oruche2025modegraph} builds a multi-hop dependency graph as a prompt. These approaches motivate two empirical questions: whether topologically executed graph decomposition helps under paired long-context evaluation, and whether explicit sub-question units support a useful content-consistency diagnostic. In our experiments, a purely structural graph-validity score is nearly non-discriminative because generated graphs are usually legal DAGs.

We study \textbf{GERS-CV2}, which combines graph decomposition and confidence-aware aggregation with \textbf{bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking}. After the forward pass produces answer $A$, each sub-question is independently re-answered using $A$ and the context as additional conditioning information. Forward/backward agreement is aggregated into a content self-consistency score. In the evaluated single-path configuration this score is diagnostic: it is computed after answer generation and does not revise or select the final answer. The DAG nevertheless provides named sub-question units on which this diagnostic can operate.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=0pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \emph{Paired long-context evaluation.} Nominal paired tests favor GERS-CV2 on two LongBench subsets, while three additional subsets and HotpotQA characterize settings where the advantage is absent or inconclusive.
\item \emph{Controlled Oracle interventions.} On MuSiQue 4-hop, gold decomposition improves the same pipeline over model decomposition by +0.091 F1 and exceeds CoT-SC by +0.075 F1, estimating the headroom available from more accurate decomposition.
\item \emph{A content-consistency diagnostic.} Bidirectional cross-checking raises correct/wrong discrimination from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$ and AUROC from 0.498 to 0.589. We explicitly document that this score is not a correctness verifier and is not the causal source of the single-path end-task gains.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\noindent\textbf{Chain-of-Thought reasoning.} \citet{wei2022cot} introduced CoT prompting; \citet{wang2023sc} proposed Self-Consistency (CoT-SC), whose equal-weight majority vote does not exploit path quality. \citet{yao2023tot} proposed Tree of Thoughts (ToT) with search over thought states, without explicit sub-question dependency modeling. These methods are based on linear or tree structures.

\noindent\textbf{Graph-enhanced reasoning.} \citet{besta2024got} models reasoning as a graph for merging and distillation. \citet{han2025rwg} uses a context graph as input, while \citet{oruche2025modegraph} constructs a multi-hop dependency graph through prompting. GERS differs operationally by executing explicit sub-questions in topological order and computing a post-hoc consistency diagnostic over their answers.

\noindent\textbf{Long-context QA benchmarks.} LongBench~\citep{bai2024longbench} is a bilingual multi-task long-context benchmark; MuSiQue~\citep{trivedi2022musique} composes single-hop questions into 2--4-hop compositions with gold sub-question decompositions. We use LongBench multi-document QA subsets as the main benchmark, and MuSiQue for controlled Oracle mechanism analysis.

\noindent\textbf{Consistency checking and self-evaluation.} Process Reward Models~\citep{lightman2024verify} score intermediate steps but require large amounts of labeled data. Self-Refine~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine} iteratively refines answers via self-feedback on linear text. GERS designs bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking, made possible by explicit DAG decomposition.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

Given question $Q$ and context $C$, GERS-CV2: (1) decomposes $Q$ into sub-questions $\{q_i\}$ with dependencies; (2) builds DAG $G=(V,E)$; (3) obtains a topological order $\tau$; (4) executes sub-questions in that order while passing predecessor answers to dependent nodes; (5) aggregates the sub-answers into final answer $A$ using confidence annotations; and (6) computes the post-hoc score $S = 0.3S_{\text{struct}} + 0.7S_{\text{crossval}}$. Figure~\ref{fig:framework} depicts the pipeline. The main LongBench configuration is single-path: $S$ is reported for diagnosis and does not change $A$.

\label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Reasoning-State Graph}

Nodes: \emph{FactNode} (facts/original question), \emph{StepNode} (sub-question and answer), \emph{ConclusionNode} (final conclusion). Edges: \emph{DeriveEdge}, \emph{SupportEdge}, \emph{ConflictEdge}. The LLM decomposes $Q$ into an ordered sub-question list with dependencies; nodes and edges are created accordingly. Topological sort orders execution; cycles are broken first.

\subsection{Bidirectional Sub-Answer Cross-Checking}

\noindent\textbf{Structural score (baseline).}
$S_{\text{struct}} = w_1 C_{\text{conn}} + w_2 C_{\text{cycle}} + w_3 C_{\text{cov}}$
with $w_1{=}w_3{=}0.35, w_2{=}0.30$ for connectivity, acyclicity, and coverage. On our data this metric clusters (mean 0.66, discrimination $-0.0035$) and cannot distinguish reasoning quality.

\noindent\textbf{Bidirectional cross-check.} Given forward sub-answers $\{a_i\}$ and final answer $A$, each sub-question is independently re-answered after the forward pass, conditioned on $A$ and context $C$, yielding re-derived answers $\{a'_i\}$. Graph edges are not reversed. Content consistency is
\begin{equation}
S_{\text{crossval}} = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} w_i \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{match}(a_i, a'_i)]}{\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} w_i}
\end{equation}
where $i\in\{0,\ldots,n-1\}$ and $w_i = 0.5 + 0.5(i+1)/n$, assigning more weight to later nodes because they integrate more preceding reasoning and are closer to the final answer. The binary match function returns 1 when normalized exact match, substring containment, or normalized numeric equality holds, and 0 otherwise. The prompt instructs the model to derive from context rather than copy $A$ to reduce confirmation bias.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/image3}
\caption{Bidirectional sub-answer diagnostic. After the forward answer is fixed, each sub-question is re-answered using $A$ and the context; agreement yields a post-hoc content-consistency score.}
\label{fig:crosscheck}
\end{figure}

\noindent\textbf{Confidence-weighted aggregation.} Each sub-answer is scored by a lightweight heuristic (non-emptiness, absence of uncertainty markers, entity grounding in context, pure-number check); sub-answers below $\theta_{\text{conf}}{=}0.5$ are tagged \texttt{[LOW CONFIDENCE]} in the aggregation prompt.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\noindent\textbf{LongBench English QA.} We evaluate \texttt{multifieldqa\_en} (100 attempted examples), \texttt{musique} (200 attempted examples), and \texttt{narrativeqa} (100 attempted examples), then extend the exploratory analysis with \texttt{qasper} and \texttt{2wikimqa}. The first two produce positive nominal paired results; \texttt{narrativeqa} has high failure rates and is treated as inconclusive.

\noindent\textbf{Mechanism benchmark.} MuSiQue~\citep{trivedi2022musique} 4-hop subset (first 200 samples with hop=4 from a seed=42 shuffle of the \texttt{musique\_ans\_v1.0\_dev} split) for the controlled Oracle study, since gold sub-question decompositions and supporting-fact annotations are available.

\noindent\textbf{Regime-boundary benchmark.} HotpotQA~\citep{yang2018hotpotqa} n=500 (single-file dense evidence, mean $\sim$4.7k characters) as a documented boundary where decomposition does not help.

\noindent\textbf{Baselines.} CoT-SC ($N{=}3$, majority vote) is the primary paired-comparison baseline. Standard CoT and Zero-Shot appear in ablations.

\noindent\textbf{Model and protocol.} We use Qwen3-8B through the DashScope API with four workers. Both methods receive the same full LongBench context, and all methods share answer extraction and normalization. We attempted 200 MuSiQue and 100 NarrativeQA examples; paired analyses retain only examples for which both methods returned valid outputs, yielding n=193 and n=71. CoT-SC/GERS-CV2 produced 7/2 failures on MuSiQue and 21/26 on NarrativeQA. We report paired bootstrap 95\% CIs ($B=10{,}000$, seed=42) and McNemar tests on EM correctness. Tests are nominal and are not adjusted for comparisons across datasets and metrics; differential failure rates are a limitation.

\noindent\textbf{Observed improvements.} Nominal paired tests favor GERS-CV2 on \texttt{multifieldqa\_en} and \texttt{musique} (Table~\ref{tab:main}). These results motivate, but do not establish, the hypothesis that graph decomposition is useful for moderately long multi-passage contexts; the intervals are not multiplicity-adjusted and the MuSiQue lower bound is close to zero.

\noindent\textbf{Exploratory interpretation.} The two positive subsets differ in length but both contain multiple passages and distractors. One plausible explanation is that decomposition narrows the reasoning focus for each step, even though the full context remains available. Because the subset grouping was developed from the observed results, it should be treated as a hypothesis for future preregistered evaluation rather than a confirmed regime law.

\noindent\textbf{Observed limitations.} On \texttt{narrativeqa}, both methods score F1${<}0.24$ and have substantial, unequal timeout rates. This experiment is inconclusive about ultra-long narratives under a more reliable serving configuration. On HotpotQA (Section~\ref{sec:boundary}), CoT-SC modestly outperforms GERS-CV2 under fair full-context evaluation.

\noindent\emph{(1) Accurate decomposition has measurable potential value.} Gold decomposition improves the same pipeline over model-generated decomposition by $+0.091$ F1 (n=199 paired valid outputs, 95\% CI $[+0.041,+0.142]$, McNemar $p{=}0.002$). Against CoT-SC on the 200-sample set, the gold-decomposition pipeline obtains F1 0.439 versus 0.363 ($\Delta$F1 $=+0.075$, 95\% CI $[+0.017,+0.134]$). This is an Oracle upper-bound comparison, not the performance of automatic GERS-CV2.

\noindent\emph{(2) Gold sub-answers give the largest measured Oracle improvement.} Relative to the gold-decomposition condition, substituting gold sub-answers raises F1 from 0.439 to 0.839, whereas substituting gold supporting passages raises it to 0.514. Because these interventions are not cumulative and can interact, we do not interpret their differences as additive percentages of total error. The result motivates better sub-answer generation or verification, while the current self-consistency diagnostic detects only a subset of such errors.

\noindent\emph{(3) The controlled decomposition gap is $+0.091$ F1.} Comparing model and gold decomposition within the Oracle runner on the same paired examples avoids the previous mismatch between an n=85 model-decomposition run and an n=200 gold run. \subsection{Consistency Score: Structural $\to$ Content}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/image2}
\caption{Consistency Score correct/wrong discrimination on HotpotQA n=500. Structural CS clusters around 0.66 for both correct and wrong (discrimination $-0.0035$); adding bidirectional cross-checking separates them (discrimination $+0.0847$, AUROC 0.498$\to$0.589).}
\label{fig:cs}
\end{figure}

On the HotpotQA diagnostic run, the structural score clusters (correct mean 0.6592, wrong mean 0.6628, discrimination $-0.0035$, AUROC 0.498). Adding the cross-check raises discrimination to $+0.0847$ and AUROC to 0.589, turning the score into a weak content self-consistency signal (Figure~\ref{fig:cs}). Because the score is computed after answer generation in the main single-path configuration, this analysis concerns diagnostic quality rather than causal end-task improvement.

\noindent\textbf{Diagnostic, not verifier.} CS remains an imperfect predictor: 156 of 250 samples with CS${=}1.0$ are EM-wrong. Fluent hallucinations can agree with themselves. In the evaluated single-path system, CS should therefore be interpreted as a diagnostic signal rather than a correctness verifier or demonstrated selection mechanism; grounded verification remains open (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Regime Boundary Analysis}
\label{sec:boundary}

We report documented regimes where GERS-CV2 does \emph{not} outperform CoT-SC and analyze why.

\noindent\textbf{Boundary 1: HotpotQA under full context.} On HotpotQA n=500, CoT-SC outperforms GERS-CV2 in F1 (0.716 vs.\ 0.683; difference $+0.032$ in favor of CoT-SC, 95\% CI $[+0.002,+0.063]$), while the EM difference is not significant ($p{=}0.314$). A separate legacy truncated-context run contains per-type EM results, but those values are not comparable with this full-context experiment and are omitted from the main analysis.

\noindent\textbf{Boundary 2: deep bridge-composition.} On the 2WikiMultiHopQA~\citep{ho20202wiki} n=100 diagnostic, GERS-CV2 is slightly below Standard CoT on comparison questions (F1 0.775 vs.\ 0.815) and substantially below it on \emph{bridge\_comparison} (0.524 vs.\ 0.905). An early entity error can propagate through the bridge$\to$compare chain (Figure~\ref{fig:type}). The cross-check score increases from 0.476 to 0.524 in a related configuration, but it does not revise the final answer in the main single-path setup and can remain high when both directions make the same error.

\subsection{Implementation Diagnostics and Cost}

The main configuration uses model-generated DAG execution, confidence annotations during aggregation, and a post-hoc cross-check. A legacy truncated-context ablation showed different point estimates across separately generated runs, but the cross-check was computed after answer generation and therefore cannot be credited causally for those differences. Confidence weighting changes the aggregation prompt; its measured effect was small (+0.004 EM in the legacy run). GERS-CV2 uses approximately 6--8 LLM calls per question versus 3 for CoT-SC. Under the four-worker API configuration, mean wall-clock latency was 6.6s for GERS-CV2 and 8.3s for CoT-SC; these are shared-system measurements, not serial model latency. Because backward verification is diagnostic in this configuration, it may be disabled when only the final answer is required.

\subsection{Cross-Model Generalization and Auxiliary Ablations}

\noindent\textbf{Qwen-Plus on HotpotQA (n=300).} GERS-CV2 obtains EM 0.363 / F1 0.496 and CoT-SC obtains EM 0.367 / F1 0.494 (EM difference $-0.004$, McNemar $p{=}1.000$; F1 difference $+0.002$). Because this evaluation is on HotpotQA rather than the LongBench subsets, it does not establish how model scaling affects the target setting.

\noindent\textbf{Auxiliary diagnostics.} (i) Uniform and position-weighted cross-check scores yield similar legacy-run answers, as expected because scoring occurs after answer generation. (ii) A context-only re-derivation removes $A$ from the diagnostic anchor and produces a lower CS while leaving answers essentially unchanged. (iii) An evidence-grounded variant suppresses inflated CS (0.781$\to$0.730) while preserving EM in the diagnostic sample.

\subsection{Additional Diagnostic Experiments}

For transparency and to document the design-space exploration behind the final GERS-CV2 configuration, we report additional diagnostic experiments.

\noindent\textbf{Two additional LongBench subsets.} On \texttt{qasper} (n=200), GERS-CV2 shows a non-significant +0.018 F1 difference (95\% CI $[-0.016,+0.054]$). On \texttt{2wikimqa} (n=195 paired valid outputs), it shows a non-significant $-0.021$ difference (95\% CI $[-0.076,+0.035]$). These exploratory results are consistent with, but do not establish, the moderate-length multi-passage hypothesis.

\noindent\textbf{Symmetric context-budget audit.} On HotpotQA with seven symmetric truncation budgets (n=200 each), the largest difference is $+0.060$ F1 at budget 2000 ($p{=}0.054$), with no nominal significance at the other six budgets. This reduces, but does not eliminate, concern that context-budget effects contribute to the LongBench observations.

\noindent\textbf{MuSiQue hop-axis.} With model decomposition, GERS-CV2 does not outperform CoT-SC at 2, 3, or 4 hops ($\Delta$F1 $=-0.008,-0.062,-0.022$; all non-significant). The paired Oracle comparison in Section~\ref{sec:oracle} shows a +0.091 F1 improvement when model decomposition is replaced by gold decomposition within the pipeline.

\noindent\textbf{Rejected variants (design-space audit).} (a) Stepwise NLI verification with re-answer (EASV) adds only +0.004 F1 over its Oracle-1 baseline. (b) Iterative deepening decomposition acts at the leaf layer, whereas compression occurs during the initial depth decision. (c) Per-sub-question BM25 retrieval obtains F1 0.563 versus 0.683 for full-context CV2 on HotpotQA ($\Delta$F1 $=-0.121$, $p{<}0.001$). (d) Grounded-forward gating underperforms plain CV2 in an n=20 smoke test. These negative results delimit the tested design space.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\noindent\textbf{Content self-consistency, not correctness.} Even after cross-checking, 156 of 250 CS${=}1.0$ HotpotQA samples are EM-wrong and AUROC is 0.589. CS is a diagnostic signal, not a standalone verifier. Evidence-span extraction or an independent verifier remains open.

\noindent\textbf{Confirmation bias.} Anchoring backward derivation on $A$ risks spurious agreement. We mitigate via (i) explicit ``derive from context'' prompt instruction, (ii) a context-only ablation showing near-identical end-task performance. Adversarial answer perturbation is future work.

\noindent\textbf{Cross-model evidence is limited.} The Qwen-Plus experiment is confined to HotpotQA, where neither model shows a GERS-CV2 advantage. Evaluation on the LongBench subsets is required before drawing conclusions about model scaling.

\noindent\textbf{Deep-bridge error propagation.} An early sub-answer error can propagate and invert the final answer on 2Wiki bridge-comparison questions. Re-derivation anchored on an incorrect $A$ can still self-agree, limiting diagnostic coverage.

\noindent\textbf{Depth-planning failure and future work.} Our decomposition analysis reveals that 67\% of MuSiQue 4-hop questions are compressed into ${\leq}3$-hop decompositions by current 8B LLMs. This depth-planning failure at the initial decomposition step is a candidate target for future work (depth-prompted decomposition, few-shot 4-hop gold exemplars, decomposition self-consistency over depth).

\noindent\textbf{Sample-size caveat.} LongBench provides $n{=}100$/$n{=}200$ for the target subsets; larger multi-domain long-context benchmarks would tighten confidence intervals. This is the standard LongBench evaluation size and is the natural next step for scaling.

\section{Conclusion}

We evaluate GERS-CV2, a graph-decomposition pipeline with confidence-aware aggregation and a post-hoc bidirectional sub-answer diagnostic. Under the reported Qwen3-8B protocol, nominal paired tests favor the pipeline on two LongBench subsets, although the tests are not multiplicity-adjusted and failure rates differ on the longer tasks. Oracle interventions show that replacing model decomposition with gold decomposition improves the pipeline by +0.091 F1, while gold decomposition also exceeds CoT-SC by +0.075 F1 on MuSiQue 4-hop. The cross-check improves a weak content-consistency diagnostic from $-0.0035$ to $+0.0847$ discrimination (AUROC 0.589), but it does not alter answers in the evaluated single-path configuration. These results support further study of graph decomposition on multi-passage QA while identifying decomposition quality, sub-answer generation, serving failures, and self-consistent errors as unresolved limitations.
