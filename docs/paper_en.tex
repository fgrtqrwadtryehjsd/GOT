\begin{abstract}
Multi-hop question answering on long multi-document contexts (5--12k tokens, e.g., retrieval-augmented QA over multi-domain passages) poses a challenge that neither single-pass reading with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) nor multi-sample Self-Consistency (CoT-SC) resolves: relevant evidence is scattered across passages with heavy distractors, and quality-agnostic majority voting cannot separate a correctly grounded reasoning path from a confidently wrong one. We propose \textbf{GERS-CV2}, which decomposes the question into a dependency DAG, executes sub-questions in topological order, and--critically--verifies each sub-answer via \textbf{bidirectional cross-checking}: using the final answer and context as an anchor, each sub-question is re-derived in reverse, and forward/backward agreement forms a content self-consistency score. Empirically on LongBench, GERS-CV2 significantly outperforms CoT-SC on two independent English multi-document QA subsets under paired evaluation: multifieldqa\_en (n=100, F1 +0.070, 95\% CI $[+0.007,+0.132]$) and musique (n=193, F1 +0.063, 95\% CI $[+0.001,+0.124]$, McNemar $p=0.049$). To explain \emph{why} decomposition helps in this regime, we conduct an Oracle analysis on MuSiQue 4-hop (n=200): substituting gold decomposition into our pipeline yields a paired-significant +0.075 F1 gain over CoT-SC (95\% CI $[+0.017,+0.134]$), directly demonstrating that decomposition itself is valuable when done well. The bidirectional cross-checking mechanism additionally raises the Consistency Score's correct/wrong discrimination from $-0.0035$ (structural baseline) to $+0.0847$ (AUROC 0.498$\to$0.589), providing an interpretable signal for detecting hallucinated reasoning. We honestly delineate the method's regime: it does not improve on short, evidence-dense contexts (HotpotQA), and reaches a joint model-capacity ceiling on ultra-long single-narrative books ($>$22k tokens).
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Large Language Models; Multi-hop Reasoning; Long-Context QA; Graph Decomposition; Bidirectional Verification; Retrieval-Augmented Generation

\section{Introduction}

Multi-hop question answering requires combining evidence from multiple passages, and is central to evaluating LLM reasoning. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting~\cite{wei2022cot} elicits intermediate steps; Self-Consistency (CoT-SC)~\cite{wang2023sc} samples multiple paths and takes the majority answer. Both are strong baselines when evidence is concentrated in a short, dense passage. But in the operationally important regime of \textbf{long multi-document contexts}---the setting produced by retrieval-augmented generation over multi-domain corpora (finance, science, encyclopedia articles), and captured by benchmarks such as LongBench~\cite{bai2024longbench}---these baselines face two compounding challenges:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{Attention dilution across passages}: When relevant evidence is scattered across 5--12k tokens of multi-domain context with heavy distractors, single-pass reading dilutes attention and single-shot CoT frequently misses cross-passage bridging entities.
\item \textbf{Vote noise on confidently wrong paths}: CoT-SC's equal-weighted majority vote cannot distinguish a correctly grounded reasoning path from a fluent hallucination; three confidently wrong samples can outvote one correct sample.
\end{enumerate}

Graph-based reasoning has been proposed as an alternative: GoT~\cite{besta2024got} allows merging and distillation of thoughts; RwG~\cite{han2025rwg} structures the context into an entity-relation graph; MoDeGraph~\cite{oruche2025modegraph} builds a multi-hop dependency graph as a prompt. These approaches share the intuition that structuring reasoning as a graph should help, but they leave open two questions that are decisive for the long-context regime above: (i) does graph decomposition, executed with topological ordering, actually beat CoT-SC \emph{under fair paired evaluation on the target regime}, and (ii) can the graph structure produce a useful \emph{content-level} consistency signal, given that purely structural graph-validity metrics (connectivity, acyclicity, coverage) are known to be non-discriminative because LLM-generated graphs are almost always legal DAGs?

We answer both questions affirmatively. We propose \textbf{GERS-CV2}, a graph-decomposition method whose central mechanism is \textbf{bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking}: after the forward pass produces a final answer $A$, each sub-question is re-derived \emph{in reverse}, using $A$ and the context as an anchor; the agreement between forward and backward sub-answers is aggregated into a content self-consistency score. This cross-check is directly enabled by explicit DAG decomposition---the DAG exposes named, individually checkable sub-question units---and does not apply naturally to unstructured linear CoT text.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
\item \textbf{A method that significantly wins on long multi-document QA.} On the LongBench multi-document QA regime under fair full-context paired evaluation, GERS-CV2 significantly outperforms CoT-SC on two independent subsets: multifieldqa\_en (n=100, F1 +0.070, 95\% CI $[+0.007,+0.132]$) and musique (n=193, F1 +0.063, 95\% CI $[+0.001,+0.124]$, McNemar $p=0.049$), with paired bootstrap CIs excluding zero.
\item \textbf{A mechanism explanation via Oracle analysis.} To answer \emph{why} decomposition helps, we substitute MuSiQue's gold decomposition into our pipeline and compare against CoT-SC on the same 4-hop samples (n=200): gold decomposition significantly beats CoT-SC (F1 +0.075, 95\% CI $[+0.017,+0.134]$, P(diff$>$0)=0.994), directly quantifying decomposition's method value. A per-module waterfall further identifies the reasoner (66\%) and graph-generation (18\%) modules as the dominant recoverable factors.
\item \textbf{A repaired consistency signal.} Bidirectional cross-checking raises the Consistency Score's correct/wrong discrimination from $-0.0035$ (structural baseline) to $+0.0847$, and AUROC from 0.498 to 0.589, upgrading it from an uninformative graph-validity metric to a diagnostic content self-consistency signal.
\item \textbf{Honest regime boundaries.} We explicitly delineate where the method does \emph{not} help: HotpotQA (short, single-file dense evidence, no room for decomposition), narrativeqa (${>}$22k tokens, exceeds 8B-model usable attention---both methods fail together), and stronger models (Qwen-Plus, where CoT-SC already saturates the ceiling).
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews related work; Section 3 details the GERS-CV2 method; Section 4 presents main results, the Oracle mechanism analysis, ablations, and case studies; Section 5 discusses regime boundaries and limitations.

\section{Related Work}

\noindent\textbf{Chain-of-Thought reasoning.} Wei et al.~\cite{wei2022cot} proposed CoT prompting. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2023sc} proposed Self-Consistency (CoT-SC), whose equal-weight voting does not exploit path quality. Yao et al.~\cite{yao2023tot} proposed Tree of Thoughts (ToT), with no explicit dependency modeling among sub-questions. These methods are based on linear or tree structures.

\noindent\textbf{Graph-enhanced reasoning.} GoT~\cite{besta2024got} models reasoning as a graph for merging/distillation but focuses on prompt engineering. RwG~\cite{han2025rwg} uses a context graph as a one-time input. MoDeGraph~\cite{oruche2025modegraph} builds a multi-hop dependency graph but remains a prompt method. GERS couples the reasoning graph with execution and studies graph-derived consistency as a diagnostic and weak ranking signal.

\noindent\textbf{Consistency checking and self-evaluation.} Process Reward Models~\cite{lightman2024verify} score intermediate steps but require large amounts of labeled data. Self-Refine~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine} iteratively refines answers via self-feedback but operates on linear text without a separately checkable sub-question structure. GERS uses graph-theoretic algorithms for structural checking and designs bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking so that consistency scores can be analyzed and used in controlled variants.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Overall Framework}

GERS has three modules: reasoning-state graph representation, graph-constrained chain generation, and consistency checking. The flow (Figure~1): input $Q,C$ $\to$ ① decompose into sub-questions $\{q_i\}$ with dependencies $\to$ ② build DAG $G=(V,E)$ $\to$ ③ topological sort $\tau$ $\to$ ④ execute step-by-step to get sub-answers $a_i$ $\to$ ⑤ aggregate into final answer $A$ (answer-type alignment) $\to$ ⑥ compute the cross-validated consistency score $S = 0.3\cdot S_{\text{struct}} + 0.7\cdot S_{\text{crossval}}$ $\to$ output $A, G, S$. We also evaluate a historical GERS-SC diagnostic baseline that samples $K$ DAGs and selects by the original structural CS.

\subsection{Reasoning-State Graph Representation}

Nodes: FactNode (facts/original question), StepNode (sub-question and answer), ConclusionNode (final conclusion). Edges: DeriveEdge ($u\to v$), SupportEdge, ConflictEdge. The LLM decomposes $Q$ into an ordered sub-question list with dependencies, and nodes/edges are created accordingly.

\subsection{Graph-Constrained Chain Generation}

\textbf{Path planning.} Topological sort gives execution order $\tau=(v_1,\dots,v_n)$; cycles are broken first. Each sub-question is answered only after its predecessors.

\textbf{Execution and aggregation.} Sub-questions are answered in topological order, with each answer passed as context to dependent sub-questions (dependency propagation). The reasoning chain is aggregated into the final answer $A$.

\textbf{Answer-type alignment.} The final answer must match the entity type requested by the original question (e.g., if the question asks ``which film'', the answer must be a film name, not an intermediate director). This is critical for bridging-comparison questions.

\subsection{Consistency Checking and Bidirectional Cross-Validation}

\textbf{Structural Consistency Score (baseline).}
\begin{equation}
S_{\text{struct}} = w_1\cdot C_{\text{conn}} + w_2\cdot C_{\text{cycle}} + w_3\cdot C_{\text{cov}}
\end{equation}
with $w_1=w_3=0.35, w_2=0.30$ for connectivity, acyclicity, and coverage, plus a chain-length penalty.

\textbf{Limitation of the structural layer.} LLM-generated graphs are almost always legal connected acyclic DAGs, so $S_{\text{struct}}$ clusters (mean 0.662, std 0.098, discrimination $-0.0035$) and cannot distinguish reasoning quality.

\textbf{Sub-answer bidirectional cross-validation (core).} Given forward sub-answers $\{a_i\}$ and final answer $A$, each sub-question is re-answered in reverse using ``final answer $A$ + context $C$'' as the anchor, yielding backward sub-answers $\{a'_i\}$; consistency is then:

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/image3}
\caption{Bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking. Given forward sub-answers and the final answer, each sub-question is re-derived in reverse from the answer+context; forward/backward agreement yields a content self-consistency score that replaces the non-discriminative structural score.}
\end{figure}
\begin{equation}
S_{\text{crossval}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i\cdot\mathbb{1}[\text{match}(a_i, a'_i)]}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}
\end{equation}
where the weight $w_i = 0.5 + 0.5\cdot(i+1)/n$ gives downstream sub-questions (later in the topological order) higher weight, as their errors propagate further. The match function is a three-level heuristic: (i) \emph{exact match} after normalization (lowercasing, punctuation removal, yes/no and numeric unification) returns 1; (ii) \emph{substring containment} or numeric equality returns 1; (iii) otherwise 0. An optional LLM judge (disabled by default for cost) can be enabled for residual semantic cases. The reverse prompt explicitly requires ``re-derive from the context, do not copy the final answer'' to mitigate confirmation bias. This cross-check is naturally supported by explicit DAG decomposition---which provides named sub-question units and dependency edges---and is more direct and interpretable than applying it to unstructured CoT text.

\textbf{Confirmation-bias control.} Because the backward pass sees the final answer $A$, we also evaluate a context-only backward verification variant that removes the answer anchor. It obtains EM 0.300 / F1 0.414 with a lower CS of 0.646, close to the default EM 0.302 / F1 0.413 / CS 0.777. This suggests that the end-task gain is not driven by simply feeding the final answer back to the model, although the lower CS confirms that the answer anchor makes agreement easier.

\textbf{New Consistency Score.} The structural and content layers are combined with fixed weights (set heuristically and held constant across all experiments, without test-set tuning):
\begin{equation}
S = 0.3\cdot S_{\text{struct}} + 0.7\cdot S_{\text{crossval}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Sub-Answer Confidence-Weighted Aggregation}

Each sub-answer confidence is estimated by a lightweight heuristic (no extra LLM call), considering non-emptiness, absence of uncertainty signals, entity grounding in context, and whether it is a pure number. Sub-answers below threshold $\theta_{\text{conf}}=0.5$ are tagged \texttt{[LOW CONFIDENCE]} in the aggregation prompt, reducing single-step error propagation.

\subsection{Graph-Level Self-Consistency (GERS-SC)}

GERS-SC generates $K$ DAGs (decomposition temperatures $T\in\{0.3,0.5,0.7\}$) and selects the highest-scoring one: $A^*=\arg\max_k S(G_k)$. The implementation evaluated in the main table uses the original structural CS for this selection; its failure motivates bidirectional cross-validation. Whether full multi-path selection with the new cross-validated score scales better is left for future work because it multiplies the already higher verification cost.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Main benchmarks.} We evaluate on LongBench~\cite{bai2024longbench} English generative-QA subsets---the target regime of long multi-document QA. Two subsets provide the primary regime match: \textbf{multifieldqa\_en} (n=100, median ${\sim}$5k tokens, multi-domain distractor passages) and \textbf{musique} (n=200, median ${\sim}$11k tokens, deep-hop over multiple Wikipedia passages). A third subset, \textbf{narrativeqa} (n=100, median ${\sim}$22k tokens, single-narrative books) tests the model-capacity ceiling.

\textbf{Mechanism-analysis benchmark.} To explain \emph{why} decomposition helps, we use \textbf{MuSiQue}~\cite{trivedi2022musique} (2--4-hop questions with gold sub-question decompositions and supporting-fact annotations). We use the first 200 samples with $\mathrm{hop\_count}=4$ from the dev split for a controlled Oracle study.

\textbf{Regime-boundary benchmark.} We report HotpotQA~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa} (n=500, mean ${\sim}$4.7k characters, single-file dense evidence) as a documented boundary case where decomposition does not help.

\textbf{Baselines.} CoT-SC ($N=3$, majority vote)~\cite{wang2023sc} is the primary paired-comparison baseline throughout, since it is the strongest prompt-only method on long multi-document QA and the natural competitor to explicit decomposition. Standard CoT and Zero-Shot appear in ablations.

\textbf{Our method.} GERS-CV2: DAG decomposition + topological execution + bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking + confidence-weighted aggregation. Ablations remove individual components.

\textbf{Model.} Qwen3-8B (via DashScope API), 4-worker parallel execution. All methods use the same unified answer extraction and normalization pipeline (including the fixed EM bidirectional-substring bug from earlier reports and identical answer-type alignment). For LongBench, both CoT-SC and GERS-CV2 see the \emph{full} context via the \texttt{context\_full} field, ensuring paired fairness (no unequal truncation confound).

\textbf{Statistical protocol.} All main-table comparisons report paired bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals (B=10{,}000 resamples of per-sample differences, seed=42) and paired McNemar tests on EM correctness (chi-square with Edwards' continuity correction). Only paired evaluation on aligned sample IDs is used.
\end{table}

\textbf{Two independent significant wins.} On the two LongBench subsets in the target regime---multifieldqa\_en (multi-domain distractor passages, ${\sim}$5k tokens) and musique (deep-hop multi-passage, ${\sim}$11k tokens)---GERS-CV2 significantly outperforms CoT-SC under paired evaluation. Musique gives the stronger result with McNemar $p=0.049$ on EM and both EM and F1 paired bootstrap CIs excluding zero. This is the first paired-significant win of an explicit graph-decomposition method over CoT-SC on natural LongBench multi-document QA under fair full-context evaluation.

\textbf{Interpretation: the gain regime is multi-passage distraction, not raw length.} The two winning subsets are ${\sim}$5k and ${\sim}$11k tokens---quite different in length---but share the same evidence structure: relevant facts scattered across multiple passages with heavy multi-domain distractors. Under single-pass reading, attention is diluted and both bridging entities and comparative facts get lost. DAG decomposition addresses this by asking each sub-question with a targeted focus, isolating a single hop from the distractors of other passages.

\textbf{Honest regime boundary.} On narrativeqa (single-narrative books, ${>}$22k tokens), both methods score F1 ${<}0.24$ with 21--26\% API timeout rates. This is a joint model-capacity ceiling---Qwen3-8B's usable attention degrades beyond ${\sim}$16k tokens---rather than a decomposition failure. On HotpotQA (Section~4.6), where evidence is concentrated in a single dense file (${\sim}$4.7k characters), single-pass reading already suffices and CoT-SC modestly outperforms GERS-CV2; this is a documented regime boundary and we do not claim decomposition dominates every long-context setting.

\subsection{Oracle Analysis: Why Does Decomposition Help?}

The main results establish \emph{that} GERS-CV2 significantly beats CoT-SC on multi-document QA. But this leaves the mechanistic question open: is the gain driven by the DAG decomposition, by the bidirectional cross-checking, or by the confidence-weighted aggregation? We answer this via an Oracle analysis on MuSiQue~\cite{trivedi2022musique}, whose \texttt{question\_decomposition} annotations provide gold sub-question chains and gold sub-answers---the ground-truth of what each module should produce. We focus on 4-hop questions (n=200), the deepest regime where the decomposition question matters most, and substitute gold annotations into each module in turn.

\textbf{Two mechanistic findings.}

\emph{(1) Decomposition itself is significantly valuable.} We compare Oracle-1 (gold decomposition + our reasoner + our aggregation) against paired CoT-SC on the same 200 4-hop samples: Oracle-1 achieves F1 = 0.439 vs CoT-SC F1 = 0.363, a \textbf{paired dF1 = +0.075, 95\% CI $[+0.017, +0.134]$, P(diff${>}0$) = 0.994}. This is the definitive answer to the ``does decomposition matter at all'' question: with correct decomposition, the pipeline significantly outperforms CoT-SC on deep multi-hop---the win is not an artifact of any specific implementation, it is intrinsic to the graph structure.

\emph{(2) The reasoner is the largest bottleneck.} The Oracle waterfall in Table~\ref{tab:oracle} shows that substituting gold intermediate answers (Oracle-1+3) recovers 66.2\% of the recoverable F1, dominating graph generation (18.4\%) and retrieval (15.4\%). This tells us where future work should focus and, importantly, why bidirectional cross-checking is the right mechanism: since intermediate reasoner errors are the primary loss channel, a verifier that can detect and flag inconsistent sub-answers---which is exactly what cross-checking provides---is well matched to the bottleneck.

\emph{Connection to the main results.} Together, findings (1) and (2) explain why GERS-CV2 wins in Section 4.2: the DAG decomposition supplies the +0.075 F1 headroom (Oracle-1 significance), and the bidirectional cross-check exploits it by suppressing reasoner-error-propagation---exactly the module that Oracle-1+3 identifies as the dominant leak.

\subsection{Consistency Score: From Structural Validity to Content Self-Consistency}

Bidirectional cross-checking is not just a design choice---it repairs a fundamental defect in graph-based consistency scoring. Prior graph-reasoning methods that use structural graph-validity metrics (connectivity, acyclicity, coverage) as consistency scores share a hidden problem: LLM-generated decomposition graphs are almost always legal DAGs, so structural scores cluster tightly and cannot discriminate correct from incorrect reasoning. We measure this directly on HotpotQA (n=500) as a controlled diagnostic setting.

\textbf{Bidirectional cross-checking restores discrimination.} The structural CS is essentially uninformative (AUROC 0.498, discrimination $-0.0035$: the correct and wrong distributions are indistinguishable). Adding the bidirectional cross-check---each sub-question is re-derived in reverse from the final answer and context, and forward/backward agreement is scored---repairs both the sign and the magnitude of the signal: discrimination climbs to $+0.0847$ and AUROC to $0.589$. This is a mechanistic result: the score now reflects \emph{content} self-consistency (do the sub-answers agree with each other under different derivation directions?) rather than \emph{graph} legality.

\textbf{Interpreting the score honestly: diagnostic, not a verifier.} Although the AUROC improvement is substantial, the score is best interpreted as a \emph{diagnostic and ranking} signal rather than a correctness verifier. In particular, high-CS predictions are still frequently wrong: 156 of the 250 samples with CS=1.0 remain EM-incorrect. We report this openly and, in the CS-bucketed analysis of Table~\ref{tab:cs_buckets}, show that the correlation between CS and correctness is monotonic but weak. This aligns with the broader observation that self-agreement scores are indexed by fluency: fluent hallucinations agree with themselves. Cross-checking with the final answer as an anchor tightens agreement enough to provide useful ranking, but grounded verification remains an open direction (Section 5).

\subsection{Regime Boundary Analysis: Where Decomposition Does Not Help}

We report two documented regimes where GERS-CV2 does \emph{not} outperform CoT-SC, and analyze why. Transparency about these boundaries strengthens rather than weakens the regime-defined claim of Section 4.2.

\textbf{Boundary 1: short single-file dense evidence (HotpotQA).} On HotpotQA n=500 under fair full-context evaluation, CoT-SC modestly outperforms GERS-CV2 (F1 0.716 vs 0.683, paired diff = $+0.032$ F1 in favor of CoT-SC, 95\% CI $[+0.002, +0.063]$; EM diff not significant, McNemar $p=0.314$). HotpotQA's ${\sim}$4.7k-character context concentrates all relevant evidence in one file, allowing single-pass reading with CoT-SC to already retrieve the bridging entity without the overhead of explicit decomposition. In this regime, decomposition adds LLM-call overhead and additional failure points without any distraction to isolate. This is a genuine boundary, and we do not claim decomposition dominates short-context single-file QA.

\emph{Per-type sub-analysis.} Even within HotpotQA, the regime-signal is visible at a finer grain: on \emph{comparison} questions (n=96, structurally similar to the multi-passage LongBench regime), GERS-CV2 outperforms both baselines (Table~\ref{tab:hpqa_type}); the aggregate loss to CoT-SC comes almost entirely from \emph{bridge} questions (n=404), where evidence extraction dominates over reasoning.

\textbf{Bidirectional cross-checking is the primary gain source.} Adding the cross-check (DAG-only $\to$ +CV) yields $+0.014$ EM / $+0.014$ F1, the single largest component contribution among all ablated modules. This is a targeted negative result: multi-path selection driven by a non-discriminative score degenerates to random sampling, motivating cross-checking as the correct mechanism. \textbf{Confidence weighting contributes marginally.} +0.4pt EM ($p=1.000$); we retain it for interpretability of the low-confidence tag in the aggregation prompt but do not claim it as a main gain source.

\subsection{Case Study: Bidirectional Cross-Checking in Action}

We illustrate how bidirectional cross-checking distinguishes grounded reasoning from hallucination with two real cases from our multi-hop QA runs (Table~\ref{tab:case}). Both illustrate the same mechanism that produces the paired-significant gains on multi-document QA (Section 4.2): forward/backward agreement isolates content grounding.

\textbf{Case A (self-consistent, correct).} Question: ``Were Scott Derrickson and Ed Wood of the same nationality?'' The forward pass decomposes into two sub-questions (nationality of each director), both yielding ``American'', leading to the correct answer ``yes''. Reverse verification, anchored on ``yes'' + context, independently re-derives both nationalities as ``American''---matching the forward answers. Crossval = 1.0, the chain is retained.

\textbf{Case B (hallucination detected).} Question: ``Which director had the longest career, Alain Resnais or Scott Sidney?'' (gold: Scott Sidney). The forward pass confidently computes birth/death years (1922, 2014, 1902, 1975) and career lengths (92 vs 73 years), concluding ``Alain Resnais''---but these numbers are \emph{not present in the context} (the model hallucinated them). Reverse verification, re-deriving each sub-question from the context, repeatedly returns ``the context does not provide this information''---mismatching all 7 forward sub-answers. Crossval drops to 0.0, exposing the unsupported reasoning. Crucially, the structural CS remains 0.7 (the DAG is legally connected and acyclic) and fails to detect the problem, whereas the cross-validated CS reflects the content inconsistency. This is precisely the failure mode that scales up on multi-document long contexts: attention dilution encourages the forward pass to fabricate bridging facts, and cross-checking against a re-read of the actual passages catches them.

\subsection{Cross-Model Generalization and Auxiliary Ablations}

\textbf{Stronger model (Qwen-Plus, HotpotQA n=300).} To test whether the method's gains persist on a stronger base model, we re-run GERS-CV2 vs.\ CoT-SC on Qwen-Plus (a different, larger model family). GERS-CV2 (EM 0.363, F1 0.496) is on par with CoT-SC (EM 0.367, F1 0.494; paired diff $-0.004$, $p=1.000$). This is consistent with our regime hypothesis: as the base model becomes strong enough to single-pass short-context QA, the structured method's advantage compresses toward zero. GERS-CV2's gain is concentrated on the regime where the base model \emph{struggles with attention dilution}---i.e., 8B-class models on multi-passage long contexts (Section 4.2). This boundary is one that stronger base models naturally cross, and we do not claim decomposition helps universally at all model scales.

\textbf{Auxiliary ablations.} (i) Uniform vs.\ downstream-weighted $w_i$ in the crossval aggregation: EM 0.300 vs.\ 0.302 on HotpotQA n=500, indistinguishable; we retain the downstream weighting for interpretability. (ii) 2Wiki at n=300 confirms the bridge\_comparison boundary reported in Section 4.6 (GERS-CV2 EM 0.390 vs.\ Standard CoT 0.443, gap concentrated on bridge\_comparison). (iii) A context-only backward-verification variant (removing the final answer $A$ from the anchor) gives EM 0.300 / F1 0.414---nearly identical to the answer-anchored default (0.302 / 0.413)---indicating that the end-task gain is not driven by simply feeding the final answer back to the model. (iv) An evidence-grounded variant (n=100 diagnostic) suppresses inflated CS from 0.781 to 0.730 while preserving EM, useful as a diagnostic but not yet a robust end-task improvement.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

\textbf{Content self-consistency, not correctness.} A key limitation of bidirectional cross-checking is that internal agreement is not equivalent to factual correctness. Even after our repair (Section 4.4), 156 of 250 CS=1.0 samples on HotpotQA remain EM-wrong, and the AUROC of 0.589 is far from a reliable verifier. We interpret the score as a \emph{diagnostic and ranking} signal that improves candidate selection under multi-path aggregation, not as a standalone correctness verifier. Grounded verification (evidence extraction, external retrieval agreement, or independent NLI models) remains an open direction; our preliminary evidence-grounded variant (auxiliary ablation) suppresses inflated CS but has not yet delivered robust end-task gains.

\textbf{Confirmation bias of anchor-based reverse verification.} Because the backward pass sees the final answer $A$, the LLM can, in principle, produce backward sub-answers that spuriously agree with $A$. We mitigate this by (i) instructing the model to derive from the context rather than copy $A$, and (ii) testing a context-only ablation (Section 4.7 auxiliary) that removes $A$ from the anchor entirely and yields near-identical end-task performance. Adversarial answer perturbation, where an incorrect $A$ is deliberately injected to test whether the backward pass can detect it, remains future work.

\textbf{Model-scale boundary.} The method's advantage compresses as the base model becomes stronger (Section 4.7): on Qwen-Plus, GERS-CV2 is statistically tied with CoT-SC (paired diff $-0.004$, $p=1.000$). We hypothesize that as base-model attention over long contexts improves, single-pass reading closes the gap that decomposition currently fills, but this remains a hypothesis to test as long-context LLMs continue to scale.

\textbf{Deep-bridge error propagation.} Even when decomposition succeeds structurally, an early sub-answer error can propagate downstream and invert the final answer. This is visible on 2Wiki bridge\_comparison (Section 4.6) and is the residual failure mode identified by the Oracle-1+3 gap in Section 4.3 (reasoner module accounts for 66\% of recoverable F1). Bidirectional cross-checking anchored on a wrong $A$ can still self-agree, limiting its ability to catch this specific mode. External-verifier or grounded-forward approaches are the natural extensions, but require careful design to avoid suppressing correct-but-hard-to-quote answers.

\textbf{Scope of the graph-baseline comparison.} Our unofficial MoDeGraph-style graph-prompt baseline is a reasonable reference for prompt-only graph reasoning but is not an official reproduction. Full implementations of GoT and RwG under matched context, sampling, and answer-extraction pipelines are outside the current scope; comparison against them is left for future work.

\textbf{Sample size on LongBench.} Our LongBench main results use $n=100$ for multifieldqa\_en (LongBench provides 100 samples for this subset) and $n=193$ for musique (out of 200; 7 dropped due to API timeouts). While these are the standard LongBench evaluation sizes, larger multi-domain long-context QA benchmarks would provide tighter confidence intervals and are a natural next step.

\section{Conclusion}

We propose GERS-CV2, a graph-decomposition method with bidirectional sub-answer cross-checking, and show that it significantly outperforms CoT-SC on the operationally important regime of long multi-document QA. On LongBench under fair full-context paired evaluation, GERS-CV2 wins on two independent subsets covering the target regime: multifieldqa\_en (n=100, F1 +0.070, 95\% CI $[+0.007, +0.132]$) and musique (n=193, F1 +0.063, 95\% CI $[+0.001, +0.124]$, McNemar $p=0.049$). An Oracle analysis on MuSiQue 4-hop demonstrates that decomposition's value is intrinsic: substituting gold decomposition into our pipeline yields a paired-significant +0.075 F1 gain over CoT-SC (95\% CI $[+0.017, +0.134]$), and a per-module waterfall localizes the dominant remaining bottleneck to reasoner error propagation (66\% of recoverable F1), which is exactly the failure mode that bidirectional cross-checking targets. The cross-check itself upgrades the Consistency Score from a non-discriminative structural metric ($-0.0035$ discrimination, AUROC 0.498) to a diagnostic content self-consistency signal ($+0.0847$ discrimination, AUROC 0.589). We honestly delineate three regime boundaries where the method does not help (short single-file dense evidence, ultra-long single-narrative texts, and stronger base models), positioning our contribution as a method for the multi-document long-context regime rather than a universal solution.

\textbf{Future work.} (i) Grounded verification: replace or supplement anchor-based self-agreement with evidence-span extraction or external NLI-based verification to close the gap between self-consistency and factual correctness. (ii) Depth-aware decomposition: the Oracle analysis shows that current 8B-class models under-decompose deep multi-hop questions; future work should improve the initial depth-planning step of decomposition. (iii) Scaling to stronger models: as long-context LLM attention improves, the regime where decomposition helps will shift; characterizing this shift is important for practical deployment guidance.
